Helvetica was designed in 1957 by Max Miedinger and Eduard Hoffmann in Münchenstien, Switzerland. It’s classified as a Sans Serif Grotesque Typeface. Originally called “Die Neue Hass Grotesk”, the name was changed to Helvetica to make it more appealing on the market. Before arriving at the final name however, they wanted to call it Helvetia meaning Switzerland in Latin. Instead they went with Helvetica, meaning Swiss in Latin, to avoid having the typeface named after the country. Helvetica was designed as a neutral typeface. The idea was that the typeface was to give no impression on the meaning of the words being displayed. It also was intended to be used for a wide range of signage.

The use of Helvetica can be seen almost anywhere. One example would be the NYC Metropolitan Transportation Authority (NYC-MTA). In 1989, they converted all their signs to a single typeface to make them more uniform and easier to read. Since then, Helvetica has been used in a wide variety of companies. Some would include Apple, Adobe, American Airlines, and Target.

Helvetica Light:
Introduced the same time as Helvetica,
Helvetica Light was designed by Stempel's artistic
director Erich Schultz-Anker.

Helvetica Inserat
Introduced in 1957, Helvetica Inserat was designed
for the advertising industry. Inserat is German
for industry.

Helvetica Compressed
Introduced in 1966, Helvetica Compressed
was designed by Matthew Carter for cold type.

Helvetica Rounded
Introduced in 1978, Helvetica Rounded is a
version containing rounded stroke terminators

Helvetica Narrow
Helvetica Narrow is a version where its width is
between Helvetica Compressed
and Helvetica Condensed.
